Core Concepts to Master
Michaelis-Menten Kinetics — What You Must Know
Km (Michaelis Constant) = substrate concentration at which reaction velocity = ½ Vmax. Low Km = high enzyme affinity (reaches ½ Vmax with less substrate). High Km = low affinity.

Vmax = maximum reaction velocity when enzyme is fully saturated with substrate. Determined by enzyme concentration and catalytic rate (kcat).

Zero-order kinetics: Rate is CONSTANT regardless of substrate concentration — enzyme is already saturated. Examples: alcohol dehydrogenase at high [ethanol], aspirin metabolism, phenytoin. The reaction rate depends only on enzyme amount, not substrate.

First-order kinetics: Rate is PROPORTIONAL to substrate concentration — enzyme is not yet saturated. This is where the Michaelis-Menten curve is climbing before it plateaus.

Lineweaver-Burk plot: 1/V vs 1/[S]. Y-intercept = 1/Vmax. X-intercept = −1/Km. A straight line. Used clinically to distinguish inhibitor types visually.
High-Yield Anki Facts
• Low Km = high affinity
• Vmax unchanged by competitive inhibitors
• Km unchanged by non-competitive inhibitors
• Zero-order: ethanol, phenytoin, aspirin, warfarin at high doses
• Allosteric enzymes: sigmoidal (S-shaped) curve, not hyperbolic
Common Vignette Patterns
• Graph showing Km/Vmax changes → identify inhibitor type
• Lineweaver-Burk shift → which line moves tells you the inhibitor type
• "Rate does not increase with more substrate" → zero-order kinetics
• Drug toxicity in overdose → often zero-order switch
Clinical Connections
• Ethanol overdose: zero-order kinetics explains why blood alcohol falls at constant rate, not exponentially
• Phenytoin toxicity: small dose increases → large [plasma] rise
• Enzyme saturation explains why "doubling the dose" of some drugs has no added effect
Memory Hook
Km = "How much substrate you need to get halfway"
Think of Km as the enzyme's "hunger threshold" — a hungry enzyme (high affinity, low Km) reaches ½ Vmax with very little food. A picky enzyme (low affinity, high Km) needs a full plate. Competitive inhibitors make the enzyme "pickier" (↑ Km), while Vmax stays the same if you add enough substrate to outcompete the inhibitor.
📖 End-of-Day Book Question — First Aid Q&A Ch. 2, Q2
Daily Check First Aid Q&A · Biochemistry Ch. 2 — Q2 (TCA Cycle / Rate-Limiting Enzymes) Hard
A metabolic process is depicted below. Identify the intermediate that inhibits the rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis and activates the rate-limiting enzyme of fatty acid synthesis.
Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA → [A] cis-aconitate → [B] Isocitrate → CO₂ + NADH → [C] α-Ketoglutarate → CO₂ + NADH → [D] Succinyl-CoA → GTP + CoA → [E] Succinate → FADH₂ → Fumarate
Which labelled intermediate (A–E) inhibits phosphofructokinase (PFK-1) and activates acetyl-CoA carboxylase?
A Citrate (between Acetyl-CoA and cis-aconitate)
B Isocitrate
C α-Ketoglutarate
D Succinyl-CoA
E Succinate
✓ Correct Answer: A — Citrate
Citrate is formed from oxaloacetate + acetyl-CoA by citrate synthase (the rate-limiting step of the TCA cycle). Citrate has two key regulatory roles outside the mitochondria:

• Inhibits PFK-1 (rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis) → signals that the cell has sufficient energy; glycolysis should slow down.
• Activates acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC) (rate-limiting enzyme of fatty acid synthesis) → excess acetyl-CoA/energy should be channelled into fat storage.

Why the others are wrong:
• Succinyl-CoA (D) inhibits its own synthesis (δ-aminolevulinate synthase) — relevant to heme synthesis, not glycolysis/FA synthesis.
• Isocitrate (B) is acted on by isocitrate dehydrogenase; it does not regulate glycolysis or FA synthesis.

Key integrative concept: When the TCA cycle is well-fed → citrate rises → glycolysis is inhibited → excess substrates go to fat. This is the biochemical basis for why carbohydrate excess leads to fat deposition.

📖 End-of-Day Book Question — First Aid Q&A Ch. 2, Q6
Daily Check First Aid Q&A · Biochemistry Ch. 2 — Q6 (Competitive Inhibition / Statins) Medium
A 57-year-old woman visits her primary care physician. Laboratory studies reveal an LDL of 194 mg/dL and HDL of 41 mg/dL. Her physician begins therapy with a drug that inhibits the production of mevalonic acid.
Which of the following is a common side effect of this therapy?
A Hepatomegaly without elevations in AST or ALT
B Muscle injury clinically similar to myositis
C Spontaneous abortion of a pregnancy
D Suicidality and homicidality in patients with bipolar disorder
E Tonic-clonic seizures
✓ Correct Answer: B — Myopathy / Muscle injury
The drug described is an HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor (statin). HMG-CoA reductase is the enzyme that converts HMG-CoA → mevalonic acid — the rate-limiting step in cholesterol biosynthesis. Statins competitively inhibit this step. (This makes them a perfect example of competitive inhibition: same Vmax if you add enough substrate/remove drug, but ↑ Km.)

Mechanism of myopathy: Statins may reduce muscle ubiquinone (CoQ10) synthesis, impairing mitochondrial electron transport in muscle cells. Can progress to rhabdomyolysis — monitor CK levels.

Why other answers are wrong:
• A — Statins can cause elevated transaminases (AST/ALT), not hepatomegaly without them. Transaminitis is a known (usually transient) side effect.
• C — Statins are teratogenic at high doses but have not been shown to cause spontaneous abortion at therapeutic doses.
• D — Statins have been loosely associated with mood changes/depression, but not homicidality. This is not a recognised clinical side effect.
• E — Seizures are not a known statin side effect.

Board pearl: HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors — remember the enzyme pathway they block (mevalonate pathway) and their two most testable side effects: myopathy/rhabdomyolysis and transaminase elevation.
Wednesday · Week 1
Fat-Soluble Vitamins A & D
First Aid pp. 64–66
Vitamin A (Retinol)
FA pp. 64–65 — Read Every Word
Source: Liver, dairy, leafy greens. Beta-carotene (provitamin A) from plants.

Functions: Vision (retinal = component of rhodopsin), epithelial maintenance, immune function, bone remodelling, reproduction.

Deficiency: Night blindness (earliest sign), xerophthalmia (dry eyes, Bitot's spots), keratomalacia (corneal destruction), follicular hyperkeratosis ("toad skin").

Toxicity: Teratogenic (cleft palate, cardiac defects) — contraindicated in pregnancy. Pseudotumor cerebri (↑ ICP, headache, papilledema), hepatotoxicity, alopecia, dry skin.

Clinical use: Retinoic acid (tretinoin) → acne; ATRA (all-trans retinoic acid) → acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL); measles treatment in deficient children.
Vitamin D (Calciferol)
FA pp. 65–66 — The Activation Pathway
Activation path: Skin (UV → D3) → Liver (25-OH D3) → Kidney (1,25-OH D3 = calcitriol = active form). PTH activates the final kidney step (1α-hydroxylase).

Functions: ↑ Ca²⁺ and phosphate absorption (gut), ↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption (kidney), bone mineralisation.

Deficiency: Children → Rickets (soft bones, bowing of legs, rachitic rosary). Adults → Osteomalacia (bone pain, soft bones, ↑ alkaline phosphatase). Elderly + institutionalised → most common group.

Toxicity: Hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, nephrolithiasis, metastatic calcification, constipation ("stones, bones, groans").

High-yield disease: Sarcoidosis — activated macrophages express 1α-hydroxylase → excess calcitriol → hypercalcemia.
Vignette Patterns — Vitamin A & D
How These Appear on Step 1
"Child in developing country, difficulty seeing at night, eyes look dry" → Vitamin A deficiency → Night blindness + xerophthalmia

"Pregnant woman taking isotretinoin, baby born with cardiac defect" → Vitamin A toxicity (teratogen)

"Breastfed infant, bowed legs, rachitic rosary on CXR" → Vitamin D deficiency → Rickets (breast milk low in Vit D)

"Patient with sarcoidosis, hypercalcemia, normal PTH" → Granulomas producing 1,25-OH D3 → Vitamin D activation outside kidney

"Elderly woman, bone pain, ↑ ALP, normal Ca²⁺" → Osteomalacia (compare to osteoporosis: normal ALP, bone density ↓ but not soft)
📖 End-of-Day Book Question — First Aid Q&A Ch. 2, Q31 (Vitamin D / PTH)
Daily Check First Aid Q&A · Biochemistry Ch. 2 — Q31 (Vitamin D Activation / PTH Axis) Medium
Hyperparathyroidism is a common manifestation of several genetic disorders that cause hypocalcaemia. Parathyroid hormone (PTH) is released in response to low serum calcium.
Which of the following correctly describes an action of PTH?
A Inhibits the production of 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D
B Stimulates calcium excretion in the renal tubules
C Induces further release of PTH from the parathyroid gland
D Stimulates 1α-hydroxylase in the kidney, activating vitamin D
E Promotes phosphate reabsorption in the renal tubules
✓ Correct Answer: D
PTH stimulates 1α-hydroxylase in the kidney, converting 25-OH vitamin D → 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol) — the active form. This then increases calcium and phosphate absorption from the gut.

Full PTH effect summary:
• Kidney: ↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption, ↓ phosphate reabsorption ("Phosphate Trashing Hormone"), ↑ 1α-hydroxylase → ↑ active Vit D
• Bone: ↑ osteoclast activity (indirect) → ↑ Ca²⁺ release from bone
• Gut (indirect): Via vitamin D → ↑ Ca²⁺ and phosphate absorption
• Net result: ↑ serum Ca²⁺, ↓ serum phosphate

Why distractors are wrong:
• A — PTH stimulates, not inhibits, vitamin D activation.
• B — PTH promotes calcium reabsorption (retention), not excretion.
• C — PTH inhibits its own further release (negative feedback).
• E — PTH inhibits phosphate reabsorption → phosphaturia.

Fat-Soluble Vitamins E & K
First Aid pp. 66–67
Vitamin E (Tocopherol)
FA p. 66
Source: Nuts, seeds, leafy greens, vegetable oils.

Function: Antioxidant — protects cell membranes from lipid peroxidation by free radicals. Particularly important in RBCs and nervous tissue.

Deficiency (rare — usually in premature infants or fat malabsorption):
• Hemolytic anemia (↑ osmotic fragility of RBCs)
• Peripheral neuropathy (demyelination of posterior columns and spinocerebellar tracts)
• Ataxia, areflexia
• Retinopathy in premature infants

Toxicity: Anticoagulant effect (inhibits Vit K-dependent factors) → ↑ bleeding risk. Can worsen anticoagulation in patients on warfarin.
Vitamin K (Phylloquinone)
FA p. 67 — The Coagulation Vitamin
Source: Green leafy vegetables (K1). Gut bacteria synthesize K2 (menaquinone).

Function: Cofactor for γ-carboxylation of coagulation factors II, VII, IX, X and proteins C and S. Essential for fibrin clot formation.

Deficiency:
• Newborns: lack gut bacteria (sterile gut) + low placental transfer → hemorrhagic disease of the newborn → give Vit K injection at birth
• Adults: fat malabsorption (Crohn's, celiac, biliary obstruction), prolonged antibiotics (kill gut bacteria)
• Labs: ↑ PT/INR, ↑ aPTT, normal bleeding time

Warfarin mechanism: Inhibits epoxide reductase → prevents recycling of Vit K → ↓ activity of factors II, VII, IX, X, C, S.
Antidote: Fresh Frozen Plasma (immediate) or Vitamin K (delayed 6–12 hrs).

Book Q10 Ch. 2 Biochemistry · Michaelis-Menten / Enzyme Kinetics Medium
A scientist working in a research laboratory has been examining different agonists of serotonin receptor 1B (5-HT1B), a G-protein–coupled receptor. Compound A has a much higher affinity for 5-HT1B than Compound B. Both compounds have a higher affinity for the receptor than serotonin itself.
Which of the following describes the relationship between Compound A and Compound B when considering the guanine-nucleotide exchange activity of 5-HT1B?
A Km for the exchange reaction with Compound A is higher than that with Compound B
B Km for the exchange reaction with Compound A is lower than that with Compound B
C Km values with Compounds A and B are the same
D The maximum reaction rate with Compound A is greater than that with Compound B
E The maximum reaction rate with Compound B is greater than that with Compound A
✓ Correct Answer: B — Book Explanation (Ch. 2, Q10)
G-protein–coupled receptors catalyze guanine-nucleotide exchange (GTP for guanosine diphosphate) of their associated G proteins. The Michaelis-Menten constant (Km) for any enzyme-catalyzed reaction is inversely proportional to the affinity of the enzyme for its substrate. Therefore, the Km for Compound A will be lower than that for Compound B because Compound A has higher affinity for the receptor. The maximum rate of reaction (Vmax) will be reached at a lower concentration of A than it would for B, although the Vmax is unchanged.

Answer A is incorrect. The Km of Compound A will be lower than that of Compound B.
Answer C is incorrect. Given that Compounds A and B have different affinities for the receptor, their Michaelis-Menten constant values cannot be the same.
Answer D is incorrect. Vmax is directly proportional to the enzyme concentration and is unaffected by the concentration of substrates or competitive inhibitors.
Answer E is incorrect. Vmax is directly proportional to the enzyme concentration and is unaffected by the concentration of substrates or competitive inhibitors.
Q 2 First Aid Q&A — Biochemistry · Enzyme Kinetics / Glycolysis Hard
After consumption of a carbohydrate-rich meal, the liver continues to convert glucose to glucose-6-phosphate. The liver's ability to continue this processing of high levels of glucose is important in minimising increases in blood glucose after eating.
What is the best explanation for the liver's ability to continue this conversion even after a carbohydrate-rich meal raises portal glucose to high levels?
A The hepatocyte cell membrane's permeability for glucose-6-phosphate
B The high Michaelis-Menten constant (Km) of glucokinase
C The high maximum reaction rate (Vmax) of hexokinase
D The high Michaelis-Menten constant of hexokinase
E The low Michaelis-Menten constant of glucokinase
✓ Correct Answer: B
Glucokinase (hexokinase IV) has a high Km for glucose — meaning it has low affinity and is not saturated at normal blood glucose levels. After a high-carbohydrate meal, portal glucose rises dramatically, and glucokinase's activity increases proportionally — it is not inhibited by its product glucose-6-phosphate and has no feedback inhibition. This makes it ideal for mopping up excess glucose in the liver after meals.

Hexokinase (in most other tissues) has a low Km (high affinity) — it is already saturated at normal glucose levels and cannot increase its rate further. Both enzymes are found in the liver, but glucokinase is liver/pancreas-specific and inducible by insulin.

Clinical pearl: In type 2 diabetes, glucokinase activity is reduced → liver cannot buffer post-meal glucose spikes efficiently → hyperglycaemia.

Q 4 First Aid Q&A — Biochemistry · Metabolic Disorders (Preview of Week 2) Hard
A 5-day-old boy is brought to the ED following a tonic-clonic seizure. He was normal until two days ago. The mother reports irritability and poor feeding. On exam: tachypnea (75/min), icteric sclerae, poor muscle tone. Labs: plasma ammonia 300 µmol/L (normal 10–40); BUN 1.5 mg/dL; creatinine 0.4 mg/dL. Plasma amino acid analysis fails to detect citrulline. Urine amino acids show elevated orotic acid.
This patient has a deficiency of which enzyme?
A α-Galactosidase A
B Aldolase B
C Galactose-1-phosphate uridylyltransferase
D Lysosomal α-glucosidase
E Ornithine transcarbamylase (OTC)
✓ Correct Answer: E
OTC (ornithine transcarbamylase) deficiency is the most common urea cycle disorder and the only one that is X-linked recessive (all others are autosomal recessive).

OTC combines carbamoyl phosphate + ornithine → citrulline. When OTC is deficient, no citrulline is produced (explains the absent citrulline on amino acid analysis). Excess carbamoyl phosphate is diverted into the pyrimidine synthesis pathway, causing elevated orotic acid — the key distinguishing finding.

Result: ammonia accumulates → hyperammonemia → encephalopathy, seizures, death if untreated.

Clinical clues in this vignette:
• Hyperammonemia (300 µmol/L — 7× normal)
• Absent citrulline → block before citrulline synthesis = OTC or CPS1
• Elevated orotic acid → confirms OTC (not CPS1, which does not produce orotic acid)
• Low BUN — liver not making urea from the blocked pathway

This question previews Week 2 (urea cycle) — a reminder of why understanding enzyme pathways sequentially matters.

Daily Check First Aid Q&A · Biochemistry Ch. 2 — Q4 (Alkaptonuria / Tyrosine Pathway) Medium
A 35-year-old man presents with arthritic pain in both knees and back pain present for months. Aspirin has been of little benefit. His joints are swollen but neither hot nor tender. The cartilage of his ears appears slightly darker than normal. No tophi are present. A urine specimen taken for uric acid analysis turns black while standing in the laboratory.
A defect in which of the following is the most likely underlying cause of this patient's condition?
A α-Ketoacid dehydrogenase
B Galactokinase
C Homogentisic acid oxidase
D Orotate phosphoribosyltransferase
E Phenylalanine hydroxylase
✓ Correct Answer: C — Homogentisic acid oxidase (Alkaptonuria)
This patient has alkaptonuria — a deficiency of homogentisic acid oxidase, an enzyme in the degradative pathway of tyrosine. Homogentisate (also called homogentisic acid) accumulates and is deposited in connective tissue, causing the classic triad:

• Urine turning black on standing (homogentisate oxidises to a dark polymer)
• Ochronosis — bluish-black pigmentation of cartilage (ears, nose) and sclerae
• Arthralgias — degenerative arthritis from homogentisate deposits in joints

Autosomal recessive. Associated defect on chromosome 3. Generally benign but causes arthritis in middle age.

Pathway: Phenylalanine → Tyrosine → Homogentisate → ✗ (block here) → Maleylacetoacetate → ...

Why distractors are wrong:
• A — α-Ketoacid dehydrogenase deficiency = Maple syrup urine disease (sweet-smelling urine, not black; branched-chain AA problem).
• B — Galactokinase deficiency = mild galactosaemia with cataracts — no arthritic/urine darkening presentation.
• D — Orotate phosphoribosyltransferase deficiency = orotic aciduria (megaloblastic anaemia, orotic crystals in urine).
• E — Phenylalanine hydroxylase deficiency = PKU (musty-smelling urine, intellectual disability — urine does not turn black).

Urea Cycle & Nitrogen Metabolism
First Aid pp. 87–90
The Urea Cycle — FA pp. 87–88
Steps You Must Know
Location: Liver (mitochondria + cytosol).

Steps: NH₃ + CO₂ → Carbamoyl phosphate (CPS1, mitochondria) → + Ornithine → Citrulline (OTC, mitochondria) → exits to cytosol → + Aspartate → Argininosuccinate → Arginine → Urea + Ornithine (returns).

Mnemonic: "Ordinarily Careless Crappers Are Also Frivolous About Urination" = Ornithine → Carbamoyl-P → Citrulline → Argininosuccinate → Arginine → Fumarate → (back to) Aspartate → Urea.

Key regulators: N-acetylglutamate activates CPS1. Arginine stimulates its own production. Fumarate feeds into TCA cycle (connects urea and TCA).
Urea Cycle Disorders
The Clinical Pattern to Recognise
All urea cycle disorders: Hyperammonemia, encephalopathy, respiratory alkalosis (ammonia stimulates brainstem), low BUN.

OTC deficiency (most common): X-linked recessive. ↑ Orotic acid in urine (excess carbamoyl-P → pyrimidine synthesis). No citrulline detected in plasma.

CPS1 deficiency: Autosomal recessive. ↑ Ammonia. No orotic acid (carbamoyl-P doesn't spill over). No citrulline either — distinguishes from OTC only by orotic acid.

Treatment: Protein restriction, arginine supplementation, sodium benzoate/phenylbutyrate (alternate nitrogen disposal), liver transplant.

B Aldolase B
C Galactose-1-phosphate uridylyltransferase
D Lysosomal α-glucosidase
E Ornithine transcarbamylase (OTC)

OTC (ornithine transcarbamylase) deficiency — most common urea cycle disorder, and the only X-linked recessive one. All others are autosomal recessive.

OTC catalyzes: Carbamoyl phosphate + Ornithine → Citrulline (in the mitochondria). Without it → no citrulline (absent in this patient), and excess carbamoyl phosphate spills into the pyrimidine synthesis pathway → elevated orotic acid in urine.

Metabolic Disorders — PKU & Homocystinuria
First Aid pp. 95–100
PKU — Phenylketonuria
FA pp. 95–96
Defect: Phenylalanine hydroxylase (PAH) deficiency (or BH4 cofactor deficiency). Phe cannot be converted to Tyrosine → Phe accumulates → neurotoxic.

Presentation: Intellectual disability, seizures, microcephaly, musty/mousy odour (phenylacetate in urine), hypopigmentation (↓ melanin — Tyr needed for melanin), eczema, hyperreflexia.

Treatment: Phenylalanine-restricted diet with Tyrosine supplementation (now essential). Must start < 3 weeks of age. Screen all newborns (Guthrie test / newborn heel prick).

Maternal PKU: Untreated PKU in pregnancy → teratogenic phenylalanine → microcephaly, intellectual disability, congenital heart disease in fetus even if fetus itself doesn't have PKU.
Homocystinuria
FA pp. 97–98
Most common cause: Cystathionine synthase deficiency (autosomal recessive) → homocysteine accumulates → cannot be converted to cystathionine.

Presentation: Intellectual disability, osteoporosis, tall and thin body habitus, downward lens subluxation (ectopia lentis), kyphosis, premature atherosclerosis and thrombosis (stroke, MI in young patients).

Treatment: B6 (pyridoxine) supplementation — increases cystathionine synthase activity in some cases. Methionine restriction + cysteine supplementation.

Distinguish from Marfan: Both have tall habitus + lens dislocation. But Marfan = upward lens subluxation, no thrombosis, no intellectual disability, no osteoporosis. Marfan = fibrillin-1 gene mutation.

📖 End-of-Day Book Question — First Aid Q&A Ch. 2, Q13 (Diabetic Complications / Metabolic Disorders)
Daily Check First Aid Q&A · Biochemistry Ch. 2 — Q13 (Sorbitol / Aldose Reductase / Diabetic Cataracts) Medium
A 52-year-old man with a 12-year history of poorly controlled diabetes mellitus presents to his physician complaining of changes in his vision. Physical examination reveals opacities on the lens of the eye (cataracts).
Which enzyme most likely contributed to this complication?
A HMG-CoA reductase
B Adenosine deaminase
C Aldose reductase
D Galactose-1-phosphate uridyltransferase
E Hexokinase
F Insulin-like growth factor
✓ Correct Answer: C — Aldose Reductase (Book Q13)
Aldose reductase converts glucose → sorbitol. Sorbitol is then slowly converted to fructose. In hyperglycaemia (poorly controlled diabetes), excess glucose floods the aldose reductase pathway → sorbitol accumulates inside cells of the lens (and also Schwann cells, retina, kidneys).

Sorbitol is osmotically active → draws water into cells → osmotic damage → lens fibre rupture → cataracts. Also depletes myoinositol, interfering with cellular metabolism.

Other diabetic complications via the same pathway:
• Schwann cells → peripheral neuropathy
• Pericytes of retinal vessels → diabetic retinopathy
• Mesangial cells of kidney → diabetic nephropathy

Why distractors are wrong:
• A — HMG-CoA reductase is the target of statins (cholesterol synthesis) — not related to cataracts.
• B — Adenosine deaminase deficiency causes SCID (immune disorder).
• D — Gal-1-P uridyltransferase deficiency causes galactosaemia (cataracts from galactitol accumulation — also via polyol pathway, but the enzyme is different).
• E — Hexokinase converts glucose → glucose-6-phosphate; not directly linked to cataract formation.

Board pearl: Diabetic cataracts = sorbitol (from aldose reductase). Galactosaemia cataracts = galactitol. Both use the polyol pathway.

Daily Check First Aid Q&A · Biochemistry Ch. 2 — Q42 (B12 Deficiency / Subacute Combined Degeneration) Medium
A 78-year-old man with asthma presents for an annual check-up. Routine blood work reveals a macrocytic anaemia. Subsequent tests show an elevated serum methylmalonic acid level. A peripheral blood smear shows hypersegmented neutrophils.
If this patient's vitamin deficiency is not corrected, what neurological symptoms is he most likely to experience?
A Confusion and confabulation
B Deficiency in this vitamin does not cause neurological symptoms
C Dysarthria and diplopia
D Paresthesias and ataxia
E Syncope and lethargy
✓ Correct Answer: D — Paresthesias and Ataxia (Book Q42)
Elevated methylmalonic acid is the key distinguishing lab finding — it points specifically to Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) deficiency, not folate deficiency. Both cause macrocytic anaemia with hypersegmented neutrophils, but only B12 deficiency:

• Elevates serum methylmalonic acid
• Causes subacute combined degeneration (SCD) of the spinal cord — demyelination of the posterior columns (vibration sense, proprioception) and lateral corticospinal tracts (motor) → paresthesias, ataxia, and upper motor neuron signs.

Why distractors are wrong:
• A — Confusion + confabulation = Wernicke-Korsakoff (thiamine B1 deficiency in alcoholics).
• B — Incorrect: B12 deficiency very much causes neurological symptoms. Folate deficiency does NOT — this is the key board distinction.
• C — Dysarthria + diplopia suggests brainstem pathology or TIA — not a vitamin deficiency pattern.
• E — Syncope/lethargy suggests cardiac or severe anaemia — not the specific neurological pattern of B12 deficiency.

High-yield board fact: Neurological symptoms + macrocytic anaemia = B12, not folate. The elevated methylmalonic acid confirms B12. Common causes: pernicious anaemia (anti-intrinsic factor antibodies), strict veganism, gastric bypass, terminal ileal disease (Crohn's).

A 3-month-old child is brought to the pediatrician for a check-up. The physician notices he has a social smile but does not hold his head up or make noises. The infant also has pale skin, eczema, an odd odour, and hyperreflexia.

Q 3 First Aid Q&A — Biochemistry · Water-Soluble Vitamins Medium
A 63-year-old man with chronic alcohol use is brought to the ED. His daughter says his memory has been very poor and he constantly creates elaborate but untrue stories. Exam reveals ataxia and bilateral horizontal nystagmus. Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome, caused by a water-soluble vitamin deficiency, is suspected.
Which of the following conditions is also a result of a water-soluble vitamin deficiency?
A Increased erythrocyte haemolysis
B Neonatal haemorrhage
C Night blindness
D Osteomalacia
E Pellagra
✓ Correct Answer: E
Pellagra is caused by vitamin B3 (niacin) deficiency, a water-soluble vitamin. Presentation: Diarrhea, Dermatitis (sun-exposed skin), Dementia — the "3 Ds" — plus beefy glossitis. Common in alcoholism and corn-heavy diets (corn lacks available niacin).

Q 4 First Aid Q&A — Biochemistry · Amino Acid Metabolism / Tyrosine Pathway Easier
Patients with albinism appear white-pink (skin colour), have white hair, and have nonpigmented or blue irises. In many cases, these individuals have melanocytes but lack melanin in their skin.
What is the most useful advice to give to a guardian of a child diagnosed with albinism?
A To avoid foods with lactose
B To avoid foods with phenylalanine
C To avoid strenuous activity
D To give growth hormone to help the child grow to normal height
E To wear clothing and sunscreen that protect from the sun when outside
✓ Correct Answer: E
Albinism is caused by a deficiency of tyrosinase (or its transport), the enzyme that converts tyrosine to melanin. Since melanin normally protects against UV-induced DNA damage, albinism patients have dramatically increased risk of skin cancer (squamous cell and basal cell carcinoma). Sun protection is the most important management advice.

Key distinction from PKU: Both involve the phenylalanine-tyrosine pathway, but:
• PKU = Phe hydroxylase deficiency → ↑ Phe → intellectual disability. Avoid Phe.
• Albinism = Tyrosinase deficiency → no melanin. Normal intelligence. Do NOT need to avoid Phe.

Why distractors are wrong:
• A — Avoiding lactose is for lactose intolerance or galactosaemia — unrelated.
• B — Avoiding phenylalanine is for PKU patients, not albinism. This is a common trap.
• C — No cardiac or musculoskeletal restriction needed for albinism.
• D — Albinism does not affect growth hormone or height. That is Turner syndrome.

Key Contrast Mastered
Prader-Willi vs. Angelman — Chromosome 15 Imprinting
Prader-Willi Syndrome: Maternal imprinting (moms gene is silent), Paternal gene is deleted/mutated. 25% of cases due to maternal uniparental disomy. Presentation: Neonatal hypotonia, hyperphagia/obesity, small hands/feet.

Angelman Syndrome: Paternal imprinting (dads gene is silent), Maternal gene is deleted/mutated. 5% due to paternal uniparental disomy. Presentation: Inappropriate laughter ("Happy Puppet"), seizures, ataxia, severe ID.
Hardy-Weinberg Tips
• q² = Frequency of the disease (AR condition prevalence)
• q = Allele frequency
• 2pq = Carrier frequency (often what is asked)
• Assumptions (Mnemonic: "No"): No mutation, No selection, No migration, Large population, Random mating.
Mnemonic Mastery
Inheritance Patterns
• Autosomal Dominant: Marfan syndrome, Neurofibromatosis 1 & 2, von Hippel-Lindau disease, FAP.
• Autosomal Recessive: Cystic Fibrosis, Glycogen storage diseases, Lysosomal storage diseases (except Fabry & Hunter).
• X-Linked Recessive: "Oblivious Female Will Often Give Her Boys Her x-Linked Disorders" (Ornithine transcarbamylase def., Fabry, Wiskott-Aldrich, Ocular albinism, G6PD def., Hunter, Bruton agammaglobulinemia, Hemophilia A/B, Lesch-Nyhan, Duchenne).
Question 1 Genetics · Population Genetics Easy
A specific autosomal recessive disorder has a prevalence of 1 in 10,000 in a population in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.
What is the approximate frequency of carriers (heterozygotes) for this condition?
A 1/100
B 1/50
C 1/25
D 1/10,000
✓ Correct Answer: B — 1/50
Prevalence of AR disease = q² = 1/10,000. Therefore, q = 1/100. Since p + q = 1, p is approximately 1. Heterozygote frequency = 2pq ≈ 2(1)(1/100) = 2/100 = 1/50.

Molecular Repair Systems
FA pp. 33–38 — High yield enzymes and mechanical repair.

Clinical High Yield
Repair Deficiency Diseases
• Xeroderma Pigmentosum: NER defect (UV pyrimidine dimers)
• HNPCC (Lynch Syndrome): Mismatch repair defect
• Ataxia-telangiectasia: Non-homologous end joining defect
• BRCA1/2: Homologous recombination defect
Core Mechanics
DNA Replication Enzymes
• Helicase: Unwinds DNA template at replication fork.
• Topoisomerase: Creates breaks to add/remove supercoils (Target of fluoroquinolones, etoposide).
• Primase: Makes RNA primer on which DNA Pol III can initiate replication.
• DNA Pol III: Prokaryotic. 5′→3′ synthesis, 3′→5′ exonuclease (proofreading).
• DNA Pol I: Prokaryotic. Excises RNA primer with 5′→3′ exonuclease.
• Ligase: Joins Okazaki fragments (catalyzes phosphodiester bond).
• Telomerase: Adds DNA to 3′ ends to avoid loss of genetic material.
Question 2 Molecular · DNA Repair Medium
A patient with numerous skin cancers at a young age is found to have an inability to repair pyrimidine dimers caused by UV light.
Which of the following enzymes is most likely deficient or non-functional in this patient?
A DNA Ligase
B DNA Glycosylase
C UV-specific Endonuclease
D Photolyase
✓ Correct Answer: C — UV-specific Endonuclease
Xeroderma pigmentosum is caused by a defect in Nucleotide Excision Repair (NER), which uses a UV-specific endonuclease to identify and excise bulky UV-induced pyrimidine dimers.

Cell Cycle & Regulation

Innate vs Adaptive Immunity

Rapid vs Targeted
Immune System Overview
• Innate: Germline encoded, non-specific. Uses TLRs (e.g. TLR4 recognizes LPS).
• Adaptive: Recombination based, highly specific. Uses T/B cells, Antibodies.
• Acute Phase Reactants:

MHC I & II
• MHC I: Found on all nucleated cells (not RBCs). Presents endogenous antigens to CD8+ cells. Uses β2-microglobulin.
